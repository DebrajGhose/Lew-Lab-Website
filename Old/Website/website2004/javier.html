Javier Irazoqui

My research focuses on the connection between cell cycle control (by the cyclin/CDK system) and the generation of asymmetry during morphogenesis.

The generation of asymmetry is manifested in yeast by a striking polarization of the actin cytoskeleton, which underlies the polarized deposition of cell wall components, and cytoplasmic components, into the bud during bud formation.

Several years of intense scrutiny have revealed that a small GTPase, which belongs to the Ras superfamily, is a central regulator of cell polarization both in yeast and in higher eukaryotes. This protein is called Cdc42p, and is conserved throughout the eukaryae family. Yeast cells defective in Cdc42p function are unable to polarize their actin cytoskeleton, and die.

Cdc24p is an activator of Cdc42p. Biochemically it is an exchange factor, catalyzing the exchange of GTP for GDP in Cdc42p, thereby converting Cdc42p to an active form capable of signaling through its "effector" molecules. These "effectors" include homologs of the mammalian p21-activated kinases (PAKs), which have been shown to mediate Cdc42p function in that system also.

Our central question is, when an unbudded, unpolarized cell in early G1 decides to enter the cell cycle, how is polarity established? We hypothesize this occurs through signaling by Cdc42p. The question then becomes, does the cell cycle machinery regulate signaling through Cdc42p for the establishment of cell polarity?

One possibility is that the activity (and/or localization) of Cdc24p is regulated by cell cycle cues. So, initially we looked at the postranslational modification of Cdc24p through the cell cycle. We noticed that Cdc24p was phosphorylated in late G1, remained phosphorylated in S, and then its level of phosphorylation decreased in M. We also discovered that this was dependent on Cdc42p, Cla4p (a PAK homolog) and Bem1p (an SH3 domain containing protein).

So we decided to test if Bem1p could bind to Cdc42p and Cla4p, acting as a scaffold (we already knew from previous reports that Bem1p binds directly to Cdc24p). Both directly, using bacterially-expressed proteins, and in yeast lysates, we showed that Bem1p interacts with both of these proteins. We also showed that Cla4p can phosphorylate Cdc24p directly in vitro.

So, what does this mean? We are not sure yet. We have characterized a multiprotein signaling complex containing Cdc42p, its upstream regulator Cdc24p and a downstream effector held together by a scaffold protein. The fact that Cla4p phosphorylates Cdc24p directly makes us believe that there may be a feedback loop, possibly a positive regulatory event. My project now is moving in the direction of finding out what this complex is doing (if anything) within the cell.
